# 100 Most Influential People in History

Based on Michael H. Hart’s book *The 100: A Ranking of the Most Influential Persons in History* (1992), this file provides short, original descriptions of each person in the order given by Hart. Each entry notes the main domain of influence and why Hart considered the figure historically important.

## 1. Muhammad
Muhammad is presented by Michael H. Hart as the founder of Islam and the single most influential person in history, reflecting both religious and social impact. In Hart’s framing, Muhammad’s role as a prophet and organizer of the early Muslim community makes him central to the emergence of a major world religion and its civilization.

## 2. Isaac Newton
Isaac Newton is described as the physicist who formulated the laws of gravity and laid the foundations of classical mechanics. Hart emphasizes that Newton’s mathematical and physical theories underpinned much of modern science and technology.

## 3. Jesus Christ
Jesus Christ appears in the list as the founder of Christianity and is portrayed as a singular religious figure whose life and teachings inspired one of the world’s largest religions. Hart highlights the enduring influence of Christian belief, ethics, and institutions that arose from the Jesus tradition.

## 4. Buddha
Buddha is characterized as the Indian religious leader on whom Buddhism is founded. Hart includes him for originating a major spiritual tradition whose ideas on suffering, ethics, and liberation have shaped cultures across Asia and beyond.

## 5. Confucius
Confucius is introduced as a Chinese philosopher and spiritual leader whose teachings became a cornerstone of East Asian moral and political thought. Hart stresses that Confucian ethics and ideas about social order influenced Chinese civilization for centuries.

## 6. The Apostle Paul
The Apostle Paul is listed as a key disciple of Jesus whose writings helped define early Christian theology. Hart regards Paul’s missionary work and letters as crucial in turning Christianity from a small sect into a global religion.

## 7. Cai Lun
Cai Lun is credited as the inventor of paper in ancient China. Hart underlines that this technological breakthrough transformed record keeping, education, and communication across many societies.

## 8. Johannes Gutenberg
Johannes Gutenberg is recognized as the inventor of the printing press in Europe. Hart notes that Gutenberg’s movable-type printing revolutionized the spread of information, enabling mass literacy, religious reform, and scientific exchange.

## 9. Christopher Columbus
Christopher Columbus appears as the explorer who sailed to the Americas in 1492 under the Spanish crown. Hart argues that his voyages linked the Old and New Worlds, initiating massive demographic, economic, and cultural changes.

## 10. Albert Einstein
Albert Einstein is described as a German-American scientist known for formulating the equation \(E = mc^2\) and the theory of relativity. Hart points to Einstein’s reshaping of physics and his broader cultural status as a symbol of scientific genius.

## 11. Louis Pasteur
Louis Pasteur is listed as a French biologist and a key figure in microbiology. Hart highlights Pasteur’s work on germ theory and vaccination as foundational for modern medicine and public health.

## 12. Galileo Galilei
Galileo Galilei is portrayed as a pioneering Italian astronomer and physicist. Hart considers Galileo’s telescopic observations and defense of the heliocentric model emblematic of the scientific method and conflict between science and authority.

## 13. Aristotle
Aristotle appears as a Greek philosopher who made wide-ranging contributions to philosophy, logic, and natural science. Hart notes that Aristotelian thought deeply influenced Western and Islamic intellectual traditions for many centuries.

## 14. Euclid
Euclid is introduced as the Greek mathematician who laid the foundation for modern geometry. Hart credits his systematic treatment of geometric principles with shaping mathematical education and scientific reasoning.

## 15. Moses
Moses is identified as a central biblical prophet associated with the Ten Commandments. Hart emphasizes that the religious and ethical traditions linked to Moses profoundly affected Judaism, Christianity, and Western moral thought.

## 16. Charles Darwin
Charles Darwin is listed as the British naturalist who developed the theory of evolution by natural selection. Hart underscores that Darwin’s ideas transformed biology and influenced debates about humanity’s place in nature.

## 17. Qin Shi Huang
Qin Shi Huang is presented as China’s first emperor, known for sweeping reforms that unified the country as well as harsh rule. Hart views his centralization of power and administrative innovations as laying foundations for later Chinese empires.

## 18. Augustus
Augustus is the first Roman emperor in Hart’s ranking. He is included for stabilizing the Roman state after civil war and inaugurating an imperial system that endured for centuries.

## 19. Nicolaus Copernicus
Nicolaus Copernicus is noted as the Polish astronomer who proposed the heliocentric model of the solar system. Hart sees this shift from an Earth-centered to a sun-centered cosmos as a turning point in scientific thought.

## 20. Antoine Lavoisier
Antoine Lavoisier is described as a French chemist known for formulating the law of conservation of mass. Hart attributes to him a central role in transforming chemistry into a modern quantitative science.

## 21. Constantine the Great
Constantine the Great is listed as the first Roman emperor to convert to Christianity and to legalize the faith within the empire. Hart considers his patronage decisive for Christianity’s rise to a dominant position in Europe.

## 22. James Watt
James Watt appears as the British inventor whose improvements to the steam engine made it vastly more practical. Hart emphasizes that Watt’s work powered the Industrial Revolution and changed economic production worldwide.

## 23. Michael Faraday
Michael Faraday is portrayed as a British chemist and physicist who discovered electromagnetic induction. Hart notes that Faraday’s experiments laid groundwork for electric power generation and modern technology.

## 24. James Clerk Maxwell
James Clerk Maxwell is recognized as the mathematician and physicist who developed the classical theory of electromagnetic radiation. Hart credits Maxwell with unifying electricity, magnetism, and light into a single framework.

## 25. Martin Luther
Martin Luther is described as the German religious reformer who initiated the Protestant Reformation and founded Lutheranism. Hart argues that his criticism of the Catholic Church reshaped European religion and politics.

## 26. George Washington
George Washington appears as the first president of the United States and a key leader in the American Revolution. Hart highlights Washington’s role in winning independence and establishing republican government in the new nation.

## 27. Karl Marx
Karl Marx is listed as the German philosopher and revolutionary who developed Marxism. Hart points to Marx’s critique of capitalism and his influence on socialist and communist movements around the world.

## 28. Orville and Wilbur Wright
Orville and Wilbur Wright are treated jointly as the inventors of the airplane, first tested in Kitty Hawk in 1903. Hart regards powered flight as a transformative advance in transportation and warfare.

## 29. Genghis Khan
Genghis Khan is introduced as the founding Khagan of the Mongols, known for vast and often brutal conquests. Hart notes that his empire reshaped political boundaries and facilitated long-distance trade across Eurasia.

## 30. Adam Smith
Adam Smith is identified as a British philosopher and economist whose work laid groundwork for modern capitalism. Hart emphasizes Smith’s analysis of markets, specialization, and the benefits and limits of free exchange.

## 31. William Shakespeare
William Shakespeare appears as the English playwright whose works, such as *Romeo and Juliet* and *Hamlet*, became central to Western literature. Hart includes him for his enduring impact on drama, language, and cultural imagination.

## 32. John Dalton
John Dalton is described as a British physicist and chemist who proposed that all atoms of a given element are identical. Hart credits Dalton’s atomic theory with providing a fundamental framework for modern chemistry and physics.

## 33. Alexander the Great
Alexander the Great is portrayed as a Macedonian king and military conqueror who created one of history’s largest early empires. Hart highlights his role in spreading Hellenistic culture across the eastern Mediterranean and western Asia.

## 34. Napoleon Bonaparte
Napoleon Bonaparte appears as a French military leader who later declared himself emperor and led numerous campaigns across Europe. Hart evaluates his influence in terms of legal reforms, nationalism, and the redrawing of European borders.

## 35. Thomas Edison
Thomas Edison is listed as an American inventor who, though not the first to create a light bulb, developed a reliable and practical lighting system. Hart also notes Edison’s broader role in commercializing many technologies and shaping modern industry.

## 36. Antony van Leeuwenhoek
Antony van Leeuwenhoek is introduced as a Dutch microbiologist who first observed bacteria and protists with his microscopes. Hart views his discoveries as opening up the microscopic world and advancing biological science.

## 37. William T. G. Morton
William T. G. Morton appears as the figure associated with demonstrating ether as an anesthetic in surgery. Hart includes him for making surgical procedures less painful and far more feasible.

## 38. Guglielmo Marconi
Guglielmo Marconi is recognized as the inventor of practical radio communication. Hart underlines that wireless telegraphy and broadcasting reshaped communication, navigation, and mass media.

## 39. Adolf Hitler
Adolf Hitler is presented as the dictator of Nazi Germany responsible for initiating the Second World War in Europe and orchestrating the Holocaust. Hart includes him as an example of profoundly destructive historical influence that reshaped global politics.

## 40. Plato
Plato appears as a Greek philosopher renowned for his dialogues on justice, the ideal state, and the nature of reality. Hart emphasizes the deep influence of Platonic thought on later philosophy, theology, and political theory.

## 41. Oliver Cromwell
Oliver Cromwell is described as a British political and military leader during the English Civil War who later served as Lord Protector. Hart notes Cromwell’s role in challenging monarchy and experimenting with republican government in Britain.

## 42. Alexander Graham Bell
Alexander Graham Bell is listed as the British-American inventor of the telephone. Hart credits the telephone with transforming person-to-person communication and enabling modern business and social life.

## 43. Alexander Fleming
Alexander Fleming is portrayed as the British microbiologist who discovered penicillin. Hart underlines that antibiotics dramatically changed the treatment of bacterial infections and extended human life expectancy.

## 44. John Locke
John Locke appears as a British philosopher who argued that governments have obligations to their citizens and derive authority from consent. Hart highlights Locke’s influence on liberal political theory and documents like the American Declaration of Independence.

## 45. Ludwig van Beethoven
Ludwig van Beethoven is introduced as a German classical composer whose symphonies and other works are central to Western music. Hart includes him as a creative figure whose innovations bridged Classical and Romantic musical styles.

## 46. Werner Heisenberg
Werner Heisenberg is listed as a German physicist associated with the development of quantum mechanics. Hart notes that his uncertainty principle and related work deeply altered understandings of matter at the smallest scales.

## 47. Louis Daguerre
Louis Daguerre is described as the French engineer who created the daguerreotype, an early photographic process. Hart views this as a key step toward modern photography and visual documentation.

## 48. Simón Bolívar
Simón Bolívar appears as the Venezuelan political leader who helped liberate much of South America from Spanish rule. Hart emphasizes his role in establishing independent republics across the continent.

## 49. René Descartes
René Descartes is presented as a French philosopher known for the phrase “I think, therefore I am” and for his work in analytic geometry. Hart notes that Descartes’ emphasis on doubt and reason shaped modern philosophy and science.

## 50. Michelangelo
Michelangelo is recognized as an Italian artist and sculptor whose works include the statue of David and the Sistine Chapel ceiling. Hart credits him as a towering figure of the Renaissance whose art influenced Western aesthetics.

## 51. Pope Urban II
Pope Urban II appears as the pope who launched the First Crusade. Hart includes him for initiating a series of religiously framed military expeditions that had long-term consequences for Europe and the Middle East.

## 52. Umar ibn al-Khattab
Umar ibn al-Khattab is listed as an early Muslim leader known for spreading Islam beyond the Arabian Peninsula. Hart notes his role in consolidating and expanding the early Islamic state.

## 53. Ashoka the Great
Ashoka the Great is presented as an emperor of the Maurya Empire in India who promoted Buddhism and policies of nonviolence after earlier conquests. Hart regards him as pivotal for the spread of Buddhist ideas.

## 54. Augustine of Hippo
Augustine of Hippo appears as a Christian theologian from North Africa whose writings shaped Western Christianity. Hart highlights Augustine’s influence on doctrines of sin, grace, and the nature of the Church.

## 55. William Harvey
William Harvey is described as the British physician who demonstrated the circulation of blood in the body. Hart credits this discovery with transforming medical understanding of human physiology.

## 56. Ernest Rutherford
Ernest Rutherford is listed as a New Zealand–British physicist often called the “father of nuclear physics.” Hart notes his work on atomic structure and radioactivity as foundational for nuclear science.

## 57. John Calvin
John Calvin is portrayed as a French theologian who developed Calvinism. Hart includes him for shaping Reformed branches of Protestantism and influencing religious culture in parts of Europe and North America.

## 58. Gregor Mendel
Gregor Mendel appears as an Austrian biologist and friar who established basic principles of heredity through experiments with plants. Hart emphasizes that Mendel’s laws of inheritance underpin modern genetics.

## 59. Max Planck
Max Planck is recognized as the German physicist who introduced the idea of energy quanta. Hart notes that this step launched quantum theory and changed fundamental physics.

## 60. Joseph Lister
Joseph Lister is presented as the British physician who developed antiseptic methods to prevent post-surgical infection. Hart credits Lister’s practices with greatly improving surgical outcomes and patient survival.

## 61. Nikolaus Otto
Nikolaus Otto appears as the inventor of the compressed-charge internal combustion engine. Hart includes him for enabling the engines that power most modern automobiles and many machines.

## 62. Francisco Pizarro
Francisco Pizarro is described as the Spanish conquistador who led the destruction of the Inca Empire. Hart notes that his actions dramatically altered the political and demographic landscape of South America.

## 63. Hernán Cortés
Hernán Cortés is listed as the Spanish conquistador whose campaigns led to the fall of the Aztec Empire. Hart highlights his role in bringing large parts of the Americas under Spanish rule.

## 64. Thomas Jefferson
Thomas Jefferson appears as the third president of the United States and principal author of the Declaration of Independence. Hart emphasizes Jefferson’s contributions to American political ideals and territorial expansion.

## 65. Isabella I of Castile
Isabella I of Castile is portrayed as the Spanish queen who, along with Ferdinand II, helped unite Spain. Hart includes her for sponsoring voyages like Columbus’s and for consolidating royal authority.

## 66. Joseph Stalin
Joseph Stalin is presented as the dictator of the Soviet Union, known for severe political purges and forced policies that caused immense suffering. Hart views his rule as having massive consequences for Russia and the wider world.

## 67. Julius Caesar
Julius Caesar appears as a pivotal Roman leader whose actions ended the Roman Republic. Hart credits him with reshaping Roman politics and paving the way for imperial rule.

## 68. William the Conqueror
William the Conqueror is listed as the Norman ruler who became king of England after victory at the Battle of Hastings. Hart notes his lasting impact on English law, language, and feudal structures.

## 69. Sigmund Freud
Sigmund Freud is described as the neurologist who founded psychoanalysis. Hart highlights Freud’s influence on psychology, psychiatry, and cultural understandings of the unconscious mind.

## 70. Edward Jenner
Edward Jenner appears as the physician who developed the first successful smallpox vaccination. Hart credits him with pioneering vaccination and contributing to the eventual eradication of smallpox.

## 71. Wilhelm Röntgen
Wilhelm Röntgen is recognized as the German physicist who discovered X-rays. Hart includes him for providing a new diagnostic tool that revolutionized medicine and other fields.

## 72. Johann Sebastian Bach
Johann Sebastian Bach is introduced as a German composer noted for some of the world’s most intricate and revered music. Hart regards Bach as a central figure in Western musical tradition.

## 73. Lao Tzu
Lao Tzu appears as the ancient Chinese philosopher associated with founding Taoism. Hart notes that Taoist ideas about harmony and the natural way influenced Chinese religion and philosophy.

## 74. Voltaire
Voltaire is presented as a leading Enlightenment writer and philosopher known for his advocacy of religious tolerance and critique of dogma. Hart includes him for shaping modern ideas about free thought and civil liberties.

## 75. Johannes Kepler
Johannes Kepler is listed as the astronomer who showed that planets move around the sun in elliptical orbits. Hart credits his laws of planetary motion with advancing astronomy beyond Copernicus.

## 76. Enrico Fermi
Enrico Fermi appears as an Italian-American physicist who helped build the first nuclear reactor. Hart highlights his role in the development of nuclear energy and weapons.

## 77. Leonhard Euler
Leonhard Euler is described as a Swiss mathematician and polymath renowned for contributions to calculus, number theory, and graph theory. Hart notes that Euler’s work permeates many areas of modern mathematics.

## 78. Jean-Jacques Rousseau
Jean-Jacques Rousseau is portrayed as a philosopher whose writings on the social contract and individual freedom influenced modern political thought. Hart includes him for inspiring later revolutions and democratic movements.

## 79. Niccolò Machiavelli
Niccolò Machiavelli appears as the Italian diplomat and thinker best known for *The Prince*. Hart regards him as a foundational figure in realistic political theory and statecraft.

## 80. Thomas Malthus
Thomas Malthus is presented as a British economist remembered for his theory that unchecked population growth can outstrip resources. Hart notes that his ideas influenced debates on demography, economics, and policy.

## 81. John F. Kennedy
John F. Kennedy is listed as the 35th president of the United States, noted for his role in the Cold War and crises such as the Cuban Missile Crisis. Hart considers his leadership symbolic of a turbulent era in global politics.

## 82. Gregory Pincus
Gregory Pincus appears as the American researcher who helped develop the birth control pill. Hart emphasizes that oral contraception had far-reaching social and demographic consequences.

## 83. Mani
Mani is introduced as the Iranian prophet who founded Manichaeism. Hart includes him as the originator of a once-widespread dualistic religious movement.

## 84. Vladimir Lenin
Vladimir Lenin is portrayed as the leader of the Russian Revolution and the first head of the Soviet state. Hart notes that his ideas and actions helped create the first large-scale communist government.

## 85. Wen of Sui
Wen of Sui is described as the founder of China’s Sui dynasty after seizing power in 581. Hart credits him with reunifying China and setting the stage for later Tang prosperity.

## 86. Vasco da Gama
Vasco da Gama appears as the first European to reach India by an all-sea route. Hart includes him for opening direct maritime trade between Europe and Asia.

## 87. Cyrus the Great
Cyrus the Great is listed as the founder of the Achaemenid Empire in Persia. Hart highlights his achievements in empire-building and relatively tolerant rule over diverse peoples.

## 88. Peter the Great
Peter the Great is introduced as the Russian czar who pushed extensive reforms to modernize Russia along Western lines. Hart notes his influence on Russia’s expansion and state structures.

## 89. Mao Zedong
Mao Zedong appears as the leader of communist China whose campaigns, including the Great Leap Forward and Cultural Revolution, led to sweeping social change and great loss of life. Hart views Mao as a figure of enormous, if often catastrophic, historical impact.

## 90. Francis Bacon
Francis Bacon is presented as a British philosopher and statesman associated with early formulations of the scientific method. Hart includes him for promoting empirical inquiry as a basis for knowledge.

## 91. Henry Ford
Henry Ford is described as the industrialist who founded the Ford Motor Company and popularized mass production techniques. Hart credits him with making automobiles affordable to many and reshaping manufacturing.

## 92. Mencius
Mencius appears as a Chinese Confucian philosopher who argued that humans are naturally inclined toward goodness. Hart notes that his interpretations deeply influenced later Confucian thought.

## 93. Zoroaster
Zoroaster is listed as the Iranian religious leader who founded Zoroastrianism. Hart regards his dualistic teachings about good and evil as influential on later religious traditions.

## 94. Elizabeth I of England
Elizabeth I is portrayed as the last Tudor monarch of England, presiding over a period of cultural flourishing and overseas expansion. Hart includes her for stabilizing the kingdom and shaping the Elizabethan age.

## 95. Mikhail Gorbachev
Mikhail Gorbachev appears as the last leader of the Soviet Union, associated with reforms such as perestroika and glasnost. Hart emphasizes his role in ending the Cold War and dismantling Soviet communism.

## 96. Menes
Menes is introduced as the early Egyptian ruler credited with uniting Upper and Lower Egypt and founding the First Dynasty. Hart notes that this unification began the long pharaonic tradition.

## 97. Charlemagne
Charlemagne is listed as the first Holy Roman Emperor who united much of Western and Central Europe. Hart includes him for reviving imperial authority in the West and supporting Christian institutions.

## 98. Homer
Homer appears as the legendary Greek poet associated with the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey*. Hart regards these epics as foundational texts for Greek culture and later Western literature.

## 99. Justinian I
Justinian I is described as a Byzantine emperor remembered for efforts to reconquer former Roman territories and for codifying Roman law. Hart credits the Justinian Code with influencing later legal systems.

## 100. Mahavira
Mahavira is presented as the religious teacher who consolidated and propagated Jainism. Hart notes that Jain ethical principles emphasizing nonviolence and asceticism have had lasting influence in parts of India.
